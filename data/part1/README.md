Building Energy Performance Standards in plain languageWhat problem is this trying to solve?
Buildings consume about 40% of all the energy used in the United States. Most of that goes to heating, cooling, and lighting. If a state wants to reduce emissions fast — and Maryland does, by law — the cheapest place to start is the buildings already standing. You don't have to wait for new construction. You have to fix what's there.
That's what BEPS does. It tells every building over a certain size: hit this energy target by this deadline, or pay a fine.

The metric: EUI
EUI stands for Energy Use Intensity. It's the total energy a building uses in a year, divided by its square footage.
Formula: (kWh × 3.412 + therms × 100) ÷ gross floor area
Units: kBtu per square foot per year (kBtu/sf/yr)
A building with EUI 100 uses twice as much energy per square foot as a building with EUI 50. Lower is better.
Each building type has its own target. Hospitals use way more energy than libraries. Data centers use more than schools. The targets reflect that.

The Maryland framework
Maryland passed the Climate Solutions Now Act in 2022. It requires:

* All buildings over 35,000 sf to hit an interim energy standard by 2030 and a final standard by 2034.
* Annual benchmarking — reporting your energy use to the state — is required now.
* Targets vary by building use type. Maryland Department of the Environment is finalizing the exact thresholds.


Montgomery County goes further
Montgomery County's local BEPS is stricter than the state. The covered-building threshold drops to 25,000 sf, so more buildings are in scope. Two penalty streams stack: a performance penalty for missing the EUI target, and a separate benchmarking penalty for failing to report.

	AMOUNT

Performance penalty (statewide)
	$230 / metric ton CO2 over standard

Benchmarking — first violation (MoCo)
	$500 / day = $182,500 / yr

Benchmarking — repeat violation (MoCo)
	$750 / day = $273,750 / yr


For a typical mid-sized public building, missing both standards can cost six figures annually. Every year. Until the building hits compliance.

Why this matters for community buildings
Public libraries, rec centers, and community colleges are taxpayer-funded. Every dollar spent on BEPS penalties is a dollar not going to the people the building serves.

These same buildings often double as emergency shelters during heat waves, wildfire smoke events, and power outages. A building that can't pass its own energy standard usually can't keep the public safe during a crisis either. The two problems are connected.

What an auditor does
A certified energy auditor walks the building, inspects every piece of equipment, talks to facility staff, and produces a report with Energy Conservation Measures (ECMs) — specific actions the owner can take to close the EUI gap and avoid penalties.
There are about 10 qualified auditing firms in Maryland for the 9,000+ buildings that need this work annually. That's the workforce gap. It's also why this fellowship exists.
